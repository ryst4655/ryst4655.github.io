The placebo effect is a phenomenon in which a dummy treatment or medication produces a beneficial effect in a patient, even though it has no inherent therapeutic value. This effect is thought to be caused by the patient's expectation that the treatment will be beneficial, as well as by psychological and biological factors. The placebo effect is often studied in clinical trials as a way to control for the influence of psychological factors on a patient's response to treatment. While the placebo effect is not a form of treatment in itself, it can be beneficial in some cases by providing relief to patients and improving their symptoms.


The placebo effect is often studied in medicine as a way to control for the influence of psychological factors on a patient's response to treatment. In a clinical trial, for example, some patients may be given a dummy pill or treatment (the placebo) while others are given the actual medication being tested. If the patients who receive the placebo show improvement in their symptoms, this may be due to the placebo effect. The use of placebos in medicine can be controversial, as some people argue that it is unethical to deceive patients in this way. However, others believe that the potential benefits of the placebo effect outweigh the ethical concerns.
